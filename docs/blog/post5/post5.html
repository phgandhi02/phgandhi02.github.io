Guardian of the Redeemer (Redemptoris Custos)

Summary:

This encyclical is basically a document that goes over St. Joseph's role in salvation history and how he is an ideal model for us and the Church which Christ has established. We see how St. Joseph stands as a silent but just figure in the course of history. Additionally, we see that his role as a father is crucial to the person of Jesus as we see that many of the prophecies are fulfilled through St. Joseph's juridical role of fatherhood over Jesus. We get many Bible references throughout the document. Many of them are in reference to Luke 1 and Matthew 1 which give us the introductions to the Gospels.

Detailed Notes:

The encyclical is split up into the following sections:

  • The Gospel Portrait
    • We see that St. Joseph has a pivotal role in answering the call of God and taking Mary, Mother of God into his house to be his wife through the sacrament of marriage. This act of obedience demonstrates a form of St. Joseph's own kind of Annunciation and fiat. This parallel is demonstrated between Mary, Mother of God in Luke 1 and St. Joseph in Matthew 1.
  • The Guardian of the Mystery of God
    • St. Joseph brings so much to Jesus by acting as his father during the hidden years of Jesus until we see Him in the Presentation at the Temple. We see that Jesus is able to complete much of the prophecies that are ordained through St. Joseph's juridical fatherhood to Jesus. Additionally, we see that God trusts Mary, Mother of God to the care of St. Joseph that he may guide and support the Holy Family even before the flight to Egypt. We see that St. Joseph is tasked with giving the Jesus his name while he still sat in the womb and registering it in the first census under Caesar Augustus. Next, Joseph handles the circumcision of his son who being born in the Jewish people is to fulfill all rites including circumcision. Jesus is given his name by St. Joseph and we then skip many years to the Presentation at the Temple. Lastly, we see that St. Joseph is called to protect the Holy Family by King Herod who tries to snuff out the Jesus by killing all the first-born children of Bethlehem. Finally, we see the scene where Jesus stays at the temple which is quickly followed by a line about how Jesus was obedient to St. Joseph meaning he was to take up his father's trade and become a carpenter.
  • A Just Man, A Husband
    • We see that Joseph remained faithful to God's call until the end and this parallels the Annunciation of Mary where we see her fiat. We see that St. Joseph maintains many of the qualities that a just man would have such as the image of a husband and a wife in the beginnings of the Gospels. We see that according to Jewish Law that the marriage takes place in two phases where first the marriage is established and then the wife is brought into the house after the husband builds it. We see that St. Joseph was already married to Mary, Mother of God before she had the baby Jesus. St. Joseph in being a just man decided to quietly divorce her based on the Jewish Law which says that he should divorce her if she is an adulterer. The Father intervenes by sending an angel to tell St. Joseph not to fear to take Mary, Mother of God into his home. We are asked to contemplate on who St. Joseph was when he followed the call of God and gave his entire life to the Blessed mother Mary, Mother of God and her baby Jesus. There are two loves which are the marital and virginal love which St. Joseph and Mary, Mother of God celebrate. They experience all the aspects of a sacramental marriage by freely and intentionally entering into marriage with the fruit of the child Jesus through he was born of The Father.
      • One quote I really liked was: This bond of charity was the core of the Holy Family's life, first in the poverty of Bethlehem, then in their exile in Egypt, and later in the house of Nazareth. The Church deeply venerates this Family, and proposes it as the model of all families. Inserted directly in the mystery of the Incarnation, the Family of Nazareth has its own special mystery. And in this mystery, as in the Incarnation, one finds a true fatherhood: the human form of the family of the Son of God, a true human family, formed by the divine mystery. In this family, Joseph is the father: his fatherhood is not one that derives from begetting offspring; but neither is it an "apparent" or merely "substitute" fatherhood. Rather, it is one that fully shares in authentic human fatherhood and the mission of a father in the family. This is a consequence of the hypostatic union: humanity taken up into the unity of the Divine Person of the Word-Son, Jesus Christ. Together with human nature, all that is human, and especially the family - as the first dimension of man's existence in the world - is also taken up in Christ. Within this context, Joseph's human fatherhood was also "taken up" in the mystery of Christ's Incarnation.
  • Work as an Expression of Love
    • I really liked how much St. Joseph is an example of true work and by his example and through the Crucifixion we have the sanctification of daily work. I particularly like this quote from Paul VI in Discourse (March 19, 1969): Insegnamenti, VII (1969), p. 1268.:
      • "St. Joseph is the model of those humble ones that Christianity raises up to great destinies;...he is the proof that in order to be a good and genuine follower of Christ, there is no need of great things-it is enough to have the common, simple and human virtues, but they need to be true and authentic."(36)"
  • The Primacy of the Interior Life
    • We are able to see St. Joseph's humility through the silence about him in the Gospels. Obviously, we know some crucial things about him but the fact that a man gifted with such an honor is left to a couple verses shows that he was focused on playing his part in the biblical story. Jesus spend more time with his father St. Joseph than with the apostles though we hear a lot more of them. Much of who Jesus is also comes from his amazing earthly father St. Joseph. There were two quotes that I really like from this section:
      • Why should the "fatherly" love of Joseph not have had an influence upon the "filial" love of Jesus? And vice versa why should the "filial" love of Jesus not have had an influence upon the "fatherly" love of Joseph, thus leading to a further deepening of their unique relationship? Those souls most sensitive to the impulses of divine love have rightly seen in Joseph a brilliant example of the interior life.
      • Furthermore, in Joseph, the apparent tension between the active and the contemplative life finds an ideal harmony that is only possible for those who possess the perfection of charity. Following St. Augustine's well-known distinction between the love of the truth (caritas veritatis) and the practical demands of love (necessitas caritatis),(41) we can say that Joseph experienced both love of the truth-that pure contemplative love of the divine Truth which radiated from the humanity of Christ-and the demands of love-that equally pure and selfless love required for his vocation to safeguard and develop the humanity of Jesus, which was inseparably linked to his divinity.
  • Patron of the Church in Our Day
    • Lastly we are reminded of how St. Joseph protected the Holy Family from King Herod by fleeing to Egypt upon command by an angel. He faithfully fulfills the demands of God in swiftness and Charity which is further exchanged later on between the father and son. St. Joseph has a similar role in protecting the Church who is first found in Mary, Mother of God. This call was asked for in a previous encyclical by Pope Leo XIII in On Devotion to St. Joseph (Quamquam pluries). This sections ends with a call to ask for the intercession of St. Joseph for the guidance and protection of the Church similar to what he did for the Holy Family.
